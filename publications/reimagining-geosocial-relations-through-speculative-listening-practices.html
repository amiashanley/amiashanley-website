    Reimagining Geosocial Relations Through Speculative Listening Practices

    Hanley, A. (2025). Reimagining Geosocial Relations through Speculative Listening Practices. In J. C. S. Castanheira, M. B. Conter, & P. S. Marra (Eds.), Sounds of the End of the World (pp. 19–26). Sulina Publishing House.

    Abstract

    This article examines the development of TerraTending: 2179, a multichannel site-responsive sound work produced through field research undertaken on the lands of the Gunaikurnai and Kulin Nations, surrounding the Latrobe Valley coal mines in South-Eastern Australia.

    Drawing on three days of embodied listening, field recording, soundwalking, and material engagement with the acoustic ecologies of Morwell and its surrounds, the project situates sonic practice as a means of attending to the enduring socio-geological consequences of large-scale coal extraction. Field recordings of human and more-than-human activity, archival encounters, and interactions with locally sourced metals form the basis of a compositional process that interrogates the shifting temporalities of mine closure and rehabilitation.

    Through this combination of site-specific methodology and speculative auditory world-building, the work proposes listening as a modality through which geosocial relations might be reconceived. By demonstrating how speculative sonic worlds arise from situated practices of listening, the article positions speculative sonic simulation as a mode of critical inquiry—one that reorients relations to land and deep time, and opens space for imagining geosocial futures beyond extractive regimes.

    Keywords

    Site-responsivity, speculative sound practice, geosocial care, speculative listening
